# Voice and Style Notes

Quick reference for the user's writing voice and specific stylistic choices, with examples from published work.

## The Assumed Audience

Picture a specific reader: a developer or someone working in tech, not a bro, someone who spends time on Bluesky and cares about design, liberal, feminist, kind, personable, humble.

The user explicitly states who they're writing for using an "Assumed Audience" component at the start of essays.

**Examples from the user's work:**

"People using AI chatbots in their daily lives, concerned with how they subtly shift our ways of thinking. People building software with language models who want to consider the implications of their design choices."

"Pregnant people, partners of pregnant people, people who expect to become pregnant at some point, and anyone curious about the experience of growing another human inside of you."

"People familiar with the _tools for thought_, personal knowledge management, and 'note-taking' space / twitterverse"

## The Anthropological Lens

The user's writing consistently applies anthropological thinking to technical topics:

- Questions what we consider "natural" vs "cultural"
- Examines how technology and culture co-evolve
- Considers who is affected by technological change
- Looks at power structures and social implications
- Connects present-day tech to historical and cross-cultural practices
- Points out demographic biases in fields (e.g., TFT thinkers being "male, white, from North America")

**Example:** In the essay on pregnancy, they discuss how "nature" and "culture" are not two fixed categories, referencing Donna Haraway's concept of "natureculture" to critique the wellness industry's obsession with "natural" parenting.

## Self-Positioning as Learner

The user positions themselves as learning alongside the reader, not as an authority:

"I am a mediocre opener trying to become a good opener."

"I'm certainly not against a slightly slower narrative opening that sets the scene before making the puzzle or conundrum clear, but you can't take too long to get there."

"I will be the first to say I don't think this problem can be _entirely_ solved by tweaking some UI elements and a bit of prompt engineering."

"Whether I can support that final problem with solid evidence and argue for it convincingly is my job for the rest of the writing piece, but at least I've begun with a compelling problem."

## Tone Examples

**Conversational and direct:**
"You would think this would be more clear cut. Surely pregnant means pregnant? Apparently not."

"To be clear, I make these mistakes too."

**Dry wit and irony:**
"Perhaps this is the watershed moment when a Twitter buzzword has 'made it.'"

"I once smashed a pickle jar this way, showering myself in vinegar, soggy dill, and tiny glass shards. I now believe it's not worth the risk."

"Syphilis is also 'natural'. And I'm personally planning on getting an epidural."

**The vibe/historical recap with personality:**
"Remember the first Enlightenment? That ~150 year period between 1650-1800 that we retroactively constructed and labelled as a unified historical event? The age of reason. Post-scientific revolution. The main characters are a bunch of moody philosophers like Locke, Descartes, Hume, Kant, Montesquieu, Rousseau, Diderot, and Voltaire. The vibe is reading pamphlets by candlelight, penning treatises, sporting powdered wigs and silk waistcoats, circulating ideas in Parisian salons and London coffee houses, sipping laudanum, and retreating to the seaside when you contracted tuberculosis."

**Personal but analytical:**
"I don't pay much attention to the torrent of AI think pieces published by the New York Times; I am not their target demographic."

**Fair but critical:**
"Professor Bell and I are both frustrated that there is no hint of this critical, questioning attitude written into the _default_ system prompt."

"Now, I have absolutely no expertise or experience yet of birth. But I've surveyed every woman I know with children, and watched one too many online videos of live birth vlogs, and the consistent theme across the board is that women both report, or clearly appear to be experiencing, previously unfathomable levels agony throughout the process."

## Specific Stylistic Choices

### Em dashes with spaces

"Language models became competent copywriters much faster than people expected – too fast for us to fully process the implications."

"They are not ancestral people frozen in time, untouched by cultural evolution. Their practices are not necessarily the One True Natural Ideal of pre-historic life."

"Subsistence communities constantly on the move — spending their days doing physical labour and living in environments filled with harsh weather, wild animals, and poisonous plants — simply have very different needs from a yummy mummy living in Greenwich or Guildford."

### Short punchy sentences for emphasis

"Strange."

"Apparently not."

"Which is a relief!"

"A bit like a zen koan."

"These people were **digital puttering** more than gardening."

### Lists of specific things

"Podcasts, videos, diagrams, illustrations, interactive web animations, academic papers, tweets, rough sketches, and code snippets should all live and grow in the garden."

"Jasper, Moonbeam, Copy.ai, and Anyword are just a few."

"bots, advertisers, trolls, data scrapers, clickbait, keyword-stuffing 'content creators,' and algorithmically manipulated junk"

"invite-only Slack channels, Discord groups, email newsletters, small-scale blogs, and digital gardens"

"I ate nothing but salt & vinegar crisps, oven chips, marmite toast, and instant noodles."

### Concrete examples over abstractions

Instead of: "People share information online"
The user writes: "Instagram stories can all be generated by patchworking together ML systems. And then regurgitated for each medium."

Instead of: "Our ancestors had different needs"
The user writes: "Subsistence communities constantly on the move — spending their days doing physical labour and living in environments filled with harsh weather, wild animals, and poisonous plants"

Instead of: "I felt unwell during pregnancy"
The user writes: "I did not expect to feel permanently carsick, chronically exhausted, and always-about-to-vomit from 6am to 6pm, every day, for 8 weeks."

### Direct address and rhetorical questions

"Before you continue, pause and consider: How would _you_ prove you're not a language model generating predictive text?"

"If you hadn't guessed already, I'm not a woman of faith."

"Remember the first Enlightenment?"

"But can't we add a smidgeon of the harsh professor attitude into our future assistants? Or at least the option to engage it?"

### Acknowledging complexity and limits

"I will be the first to say I don't think this problem can be _entirely_ solved by tweaking some UI elements and a bit of prompt engineering."

"Whether I can support that final problem with solid evidence and argue for it convincingly is my job for the rest of the writing piece, but at least I've begun with a compelling problem."

"I should acknowledge that jumping into full-on web development is simply beyond the abilities and interests of many people."

"This all comes with an important caveat; **gardens make their imperfection known to readers.**"

### British colloquialisms

"faffing about syncing things, defragging – like pruning for young people"

"Most of the books in this space are a bit meh"

"This is a good exercise in holding two contradictory truths in your mind at once. A bit like a zen koan."

"I'd like to think we can have our grossly profitable automation cake and eat it too."

"Constant babywearing, breastfeeding, and co-sleeping are less critical for survival when you're in a heated flat equipped with a Snoo, a baby barista, and a rotating selection of ergonomic bouncers from John Lewis."

## The Formal/Informal Juxtaposition

The user frequently mixes academic language and references with casual, colloquial phrasing:

"Anthropologist Donna Haraway was one the first to argue this in her 1991 book Simians, Cyborgs, and Women. Sadly, both Haraway and Latour are horrendous writers and trying to understand their original texts is a laborious and baffling process, which is probably why they're not more widely known."

"This gives _tools for thought_ a distinctly computer-oriented, male, American, middle-class flavour."

"Putting the word FUCK in all caps in your opening is a valid way to get people's attention, but you'll need to back it up with a good story."
