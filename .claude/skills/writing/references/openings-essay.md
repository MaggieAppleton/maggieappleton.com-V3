# On Opening Essays, Conference Talks, and Jam Jars

_Source: maggieappleton.com_

**This is the user's comprehensive guide on how to write strong openings. Reference this when working on opening sentences or paragraphs.**

## Key Principles

Most narrative non-fiction pieces don't grip readers in the first sentence. Common weak openings include:

**Bad Opening #1: The Chronological Start**
"I first became interested in data structures 17 years ago while pursuing my undergraduate degree..."

The beginning is almost never the most compelling part. It's just what you thought of first.

**Bad Opening #2: The Beginning Of Time**
"For tens of thousands of years, humans have relied on their fingers to do things in the world. Fingers have carved rocks, thrown spears, sewn clothes, and held Doritos..."

Invoking paleolithic people is an overplayed way to convince us your topic is cosmically important.

**Bad Opening #3: Outline + Definition**
"This post will outline the most common misconceptions in astronomy. Astronomy is the scientific study of celestial objects such as the stars and planets, and misconceptions are falsely held beliefs..."

Up-front outlines and definitions are dull for narrative non-fiction (though fine for technical docs and academic papers).

## What Makes Good Openings

**Openings need tension – paradoxes, unanswered questions, and unresolved action.**

Good openings propose problems, pose questions, drop you into an unfinished story, or point at fundamental tensions. Ideally within the first paragraph or two.

### Example: David Graeber, Bullshit Jobs

"In the year 1930, John Maynard Keynes predicted that, by century's end, technology would have advanced sufficiently that countries like Great Britain or the United States would have achieved a 15-hour work week. There's every reason to believe he was right. In technological terms, we are quite capable of this. And yet it didn't happen."

**Why it works:** Sets up a clear paradox. Our technological advances should have led to fewer working hours, but they haven't. Strange.

### Example: David Foster Wallace

"Did you know that probing the seamy underbelly of U.S. lexicography reveals ideological strife and controversy and intrigue and nastiness and fervor on a nearly hanging-chad scale?"

**Why it works:** Series of rhetorical questions where the answer is "of course not!" Also creates an unexpected contrast – how is something as dull as lexicography filled with drama?

### Example: Rebecca Solnit

"The young woman at the blockade was worried about the banner the Oaklanders brought, she told me, because she and her co-organisers had tried to be careful about messaging. But the words FUCK OFF GOOGLE in giant letters on a purple sheet held up in front of a blockaded Google bus gladdened the hearts of other San Franciscans..."

**Why it works:** Puts FUCK in all caps to get attention, gives scenic details, then points out the curiosity: privileged tech workers fail to understand why locals hate them.

## Finding Tension and Problems

Fiction writers have it easy – they can invent murders and magical powers. Non-fiction writers must find the compelling problem within reality.

### The Problem Formula (from Joseph Williams)

Problems are:
- A **destabilising condition** (change in the status quo)
- That has **costs** (consequences)
- For a **community of readers** (your audience)
- That needs a **solution** (or response)

**Example:**
"Recently, the thinning of the ozone layer has allowed sunlight to reach the earth unfiltered. [CHANGE] As a result, we are going to have more cancer and higher medical costs. [CONSEQUENCES] We can avoid these consequences only if we ban chemicals that degrade ozone. [SOLUTION]"

### Make Problems Consequential

First ideas of a problem are often not consequential enough. Ask "So what?" repeatedly to get at the underlying issue.

Starting problem: "Language models can write text that looks human."

_So what?_

We won't be able to tell whether a language model or a human wrote something.

_So what?_

Language models hallucinate and our systems for vetting truth will become weaker.

_So what?_

Misinformation will spread further, fracturing our shared reality and threatening democratic governance.

## The Time/Place/Character/Action Pattern

A common effective pattern:

`On {date} in {place}, {character} did {strange/compelling/dramatic thing}.`

The strange thing must be extreme enough that we want to know much more about how the character came to be doing this.

**Example:**
"On 23 May 2014, Elliot Rodger, a 22-year-old college dropout, became the world's most famous 'incel' – involuntary celibate..."

## For Conference Talks

Talks need more traditional signposting because audiences are trapped and can't close the tab. Within the first minute:

1. State the compelling problem with consequences for this audience, with suggested solutions
2. Outline what you'll cover: "We're going to talk about A, B, C, and D, in that order"

Give the audience a map of where you're going so they can follow your argument.
